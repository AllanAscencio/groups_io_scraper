Hypoglycemia 2
Dear Colleagues; Any ideas, I have a new born now 3 weeks of age with severe hypoglycemia. Infant was born full term, NPLD, mother without history of illness or diabetes, use of oral hypoglycmic agents or other meds. The infant, off meds, has glucose values repeatedly in the low teens; requires 16 mg/kg/min to maintain glucose. With Diazoxide 20 mg/kg/day given in 4 divided doses did better initially with a drop in infusion rate to 6 mg/kg/min. However with time, this has increased again and the infant has had multiple episodes with low glucose to <20 mg/dl. Now on hydrocortisone to prevent severe drops with Diazoxide but still a problem. Physical exam normal, no hepatomegly. Insulin levels all low < 10 with hypoglycemia. GH, Cortisol, organic acids, AA, cholesterol, uric acid, lytes, acid/base status, all normal. Does not have umbilical artery line. Pending: prolinsulin, c-peptide and IGF II (renal ultrasound and pancreatic ultrasound normal). With current treatment most glucose values above 90 mg/dl but on occasion will have a sudden drop. Have considered octreotide but without elevated insulin, not sure yet if this would be beneficial. Any thoughts????? Bill

47, XXY Non-Klinefelter AIS? 2
Hello: Does anyone have clinical experience with patient(s) with Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome and a 47, XXY karyotype rather than 46, XY? I was not aware of a 47, XXY karyotype other than in Klinefelter Syndrome. Moreover, I would have thought that 47, XXY would be inconsistent with AIS because the presence of an AR gene mutation on one of the X's would be compensated for by the other X, similar to a 46, XX carrier. I could understand AIS occurring in XY/XO mosaicism, but do not understand how it occurs in XXY. Nonetheless, there are at least two articles suggesting otherwise: Gerli, M.; Migliorini, G.; Bocchini, V.; Venti, G.; Ferrarese, R.; Donti, E.; Rosi, G. : A case report of complete testicular feminization and 47,XXY karyotype. J. Med. Genet. 16: 480-483, 1979. PubMed ID : <A HREF="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Entrez/query_old?db=m&form=6&uid=575389&dopt=r">575389</A> German, J.; Vesell, M. : Testicular feminization in monozygotic twins with 47 chromosomes (XXY). Ann. Genet. 9: 5-8, 1966. PubMed ID : <A HREF="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/htbin-post/Entrez/query_old?db=m&form=6&uid=5295702&dopt=r">5295702</A> I am concurrently addressing this issue with a few geneticists, but would like to know whether anyone on the Board has clinical experience with 47, XXY individuals who present with CAIS phenotype. On a somewhat related note, but again something perhaps more appropriate for geneticists, given the possible fertility of someone with low-grade AIS (Quigley Scale 1 or 2), is it therefore not theoretically possible for such 46, XY individual to father a 46, XX child who has AIS if the mother is a 46, XX carrier? Is anyone aware of such case being reported in literature? Any thoughts are greatly appreciated. Sherri Groveman Founder, AIS Support Group USA

re hypoglycaemia
Remember to check an ammonia (see ref below). The most practical ref I used recently is also below. Barry Taylor Paediatrics and Child Health Aynsley-Green, A., K. Hussain, et al. (2000). "Practical management of hyperinsulinism in infancy." Arch Dis Child Fetal Neonatal Ed 82(2): F98-F107. Hyperinsulinism in infancy is one of the most difficult problems to manage in contemporary paediatric endocrinology. Although the diagnosis can usually be achieved without difficulty, it presents the paediatrician with formidable day to day management problems. Despite recent advances in understanding the pathophysiology of hyperinsulinism, the neurological outcome remains poor, and there is often a choice of unsatisfactory treatments, with life long sequelae for the child and his or her family. This paper presents a state of the art overview on management derived from a consensus workshop held by the European network for research into hyperinsulinism (ENRHI). The consensus is presented as an educational aid for paediatricians and children's nurses. It offers a practical guide to management based on the most up to date knowledge. It presents a proposed management cascade and focuses on the clinical recognition of the disease, the immediate steps that should be taken to stabilise the infant during diagnostic investigations, and the principles of definitive treatment. Miki, Y., T. Taki, et al. (2000). "Novel missense mutations in the glutamate dehydrogenase gene in the congenital hyperinsulinism-hyperammonemia syndrome." J Pediatr 136(1): 69-72. OBJECTIVES: The objectives of this study were to clarify the involvement of the glutamate dehydrogenase gene in congenital hyperinsulinemia-hyperammonemia syndrome (CHHS) and the relationships between the mutation of the gene and clinical severity. STUDY DESIGN: Five unrelated Japanese patients (3 girls and 2 boys) with CHHS were investigated. All patients had convulsions or loss of consciousness resulting from hypoglycemia at less than 1 year of age. We examined mutations of the glutamate dehydrogenase gene using genomic or reverse- transcriptase polymerase chain reactions, followed by direct sequencing. RESULTS: We identified heterozygous missense mutations in all patients. Three patients had a previously identified mutation (C-- >T at nt 1506) at codon 445 in the allosteric domain. Two novel missense mutations were identified in the other patients. These mutations included a change of A-->C at nt 1059 and a change of G-->A at nt 966, within the catalytic domain of the glutamate dehydrogenase gene. The locus of the mutations was not associated with the severity of hypoglycemia. CONCLUSIONS: Our results suggest that structural aberrations of not only the allosteric domain but also the catalytic domain of the glutamate dehydrogenase protein, caused by missense mutations, can result in the development of CHHS.
